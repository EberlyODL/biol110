A defining feature of prokaryotes is their lack of membrane-bound nuclei. This is not to say they lack subcellular specialization because some prokaryotes have very elaborate internal membranes. However, they generally have less subcellular specialization than eukaryotes (organisms with membrane-bound nuclei and organelles). Also, prokaryotes are usually smaller than eukaryotic cells (1-5 microns compared to 10-100 microns). Prokaryotes are often described as being "simple" but this is not an accurate description of the prokaryotes.  While they tend to have a more basic structure than eukaryotes, in other ways they are more complex.  For example, prokaryotes are capable of more diverse metabolic and nutritional pathways than eukaryotes. 

Prokaryotes are often described as single-celled organisms, but they can form colonies that show a remarkable level of complexity (as depicted in this colorized image of a bacterial colony). The shape of individual cells is used to classify prokaryotes; they can be either spherical (coccus), rod-shaped (bacillus), or helical (spirillum).
