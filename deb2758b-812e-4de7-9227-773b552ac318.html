Slide 1
Symbiotic relationships refer to long term associations between two different species.  We classify symbiotic relationships according to how the two species are affected.

A mutualism is relationship in which both species benefit (we indicate this with a +,+).

A commensalism is a relationsip in which one species benefits and the other species is relatively unaffected (indicated with a + and 0).

Parasitism is a relationship in which the parasite benefits and the host organism is harmed (indicated with a + and -).

Slide 2

As an example of mutualism we can look at the pea aphid and its bacterial endosymbiont.  An endosymbiont is an organism that lives inside its symbiotic partner.

The pea aphid, Acyrthosiphon pisum, is an insect pest that sucks the juices from its host plant. However, plant sap is deficient in several essential amino acids.
The pea aphid thrives nonetheless thanks to specialized cells within its body that contain the gamma proteobacterium, Buchnera aphidicola, that can live nowhere else. 

The genome of this endosymbiotic Gram-negative bacterium encodes a number of enzymes needed to complete the synthesis of the amino acids needed by its host.

In return, the aphid's genome encodes enzymes needed by Buchnera to synthesize its lipopolysaccharide cell wall and has lost genes that might otherwise repel infection by Gram-negative bacteria.

Slide 3
As an example of commensalism we can think about all of the different bacteria that live in us and on us.  Some of these are mutualists that help us synthesize vitamins and avoid infection by nasty bacteria.  Some are these nasty parasite bacteria that can make us sick.  But many are commensalistic - at least as far as we know.  They live in us and on us and we are relatively unaffected.

In fact, the human intestine has an insane number of bacteria - somewhere in the number of 100 trillion.  Some biologists have suggested that the human intestine has the highest cell density on earth!

It is important to remember that symbiotic relationships can change when the environment changes.  For example, it is very dangerous to experience a stomach wound - all of these bacteria that live in our intestines and cause no problems - can become quite dangerous if the stomach wall is breached and the bacteria get into our blood stream.  

Slide 4
A dramatic example of parasitism is the gram negative bacterium Yersinia pestis - which is responsible for the disease known as the plague or the black death.

This disease is spread by fleas or through direct contact with a sick person.  

Fleas are common inhabitants of the fur of rats so outbreaks of the plague have been correlated with peaks in rat populations.  In the 6th century the plague ravaged the world for about 50 years and caused 100 million deaths.

This nasty disease visited Europe again in the 14th century and killed 25 million people which was about 25% of the total European population.

Slide 5
Today we can treat Yersinia pestis with antibiotics but it still exists and makes people sick - even in the US where there are about 10 cases per year.

Slide 6
Discussions about bacteria in the popular press are usually focused on the bad things that bacteria do like make us sick.  

However, most bacteria either do not affect us directly or are beneficial.  We rely on bacteria to help us synthesize vitamins, maintain a healthy digestive tract, and develop our immune systems.  Bacteria play an essential role as recyclers of organic material in the environment and help keep soils fertile so plants can continue to grow.

We have also learned to use bacteria in beneficial ways - we now use bacteria to make human medicines - for example, we use bacteria to make insulin for the treatment of people with diabetes.

We also have learned to use bacteria to help us clean our environment - bioremediation is the process of using biological organisms like bacteria to clean up the environment.  We can use bacteria to eat oil spills and chemical spills - this is, of course, due to the fact that prokaryotes have such diverse metabolic modes.
