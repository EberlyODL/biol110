While this may (or may not) make you proud, let's take a closer look at this study. Did you get asked questions as part of this survey?  Did your friends?  Did your dorm neighbor?

I am guessing that your answer to most of these questions is NO.

There are an estimated 14 million students in U.S. universities, and, while the survey sample size of 122,000 university students may seem like a lot, does this result represent the populous view of U.S. university students?

How practical/easy do you think it would be to question all 14 million U.S. university students? It would be a difficult task. So how do you test hypotheses if you cannot use the entire population?

This has already been alluded to in your simple experimental design where you used a subset of people to test your hypotheses about fast food and obesity—i.e. you sub-sampled. In effect, you are making inferences about the entire population based on the smaller sample.

Definitions

Population: The group of individuals, or items, to be studied is called the population (N).

E.g. The U.S. university student population in the "Party School" example.

E.g. The adult U.S. population in the "Fast Food and Obesity" example.

Sample: A subset (n) of the entire population (N).  Those people, objects, or events selected from the population for inclusion in the study.

E.g. The 122,000 students surveyed in the "Party School" example.

E.g. The 50 control group and 50 treatment group individuals in the "Fast Food and Obesity" example.

Note that the notation for the entire population is an uppercase N, whereas for the sample it is a lowercase n.
