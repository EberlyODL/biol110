What is climate change and global warming?

Climate change is a broad term that refers to long-term changes in climate, including average temperature and rainfall change. Global warming is the gradual increase of the Earth's average surface temperature.

During the past 100 years, global average surface temperatures have increased by .9°C (read more about temperature record - if you click on the graph on the wikipedia page, you will see a detailed and referenced description of how the graph was produced). The terms 'global warming' and 'climate change' are often used interchangeably. As a consequence of this and future heating, climate change has been suggested to be one of the greatest social, economic and environmental challenges of our time.

The potential impacts of climate change include temperature increases, sea-level rises, melting of the polar ice-caps, changed weather patterns (droughts and floods), and increased sea temperatures among others. If true, not only will these have an impact on species and ecosystems, but they will have a profound effect on our current way of life.

What is causing the warming?

What is known for sure is that the incoming energy from the sun in the form of solar radiation passes through the atmosphere and heats the earth's surface. Approximately 50% of the solar energy directed at the earth is directly absorbed at the surface, and the remaining 50% is reflected and radiated back into space in the form of infrared radiation. However, a proportion (22%) of the reflected infrared radiation is trapped by some atmospheric gases (called greenhouse gases), which results in maintaining the Earth's temperature at a level necessary to support life (additional information on the greenhouse effect). The problem we now face is that there is an increasing amount of greenhouse gases (including methane and carbon dioxide) in the atmosphere that trap heat--the so-called greenhouse effect--whereby less heat is escaping and more heat is being trapped within the atmosphere.

Thus, one of the big questions that we are constantly faced with in the media is whether the warming we are experiencing is a natural phenomenon or whether it is due to human influences. There are many Web sites and media outlets out there that portray different points of view on this subject, but if you are interested in more information, Global Issues provides a brief introduction on global warming from both a social and environmental perspective, and Skeptical Science provides a useful synthesis and references to both sides of the global warming debate.
