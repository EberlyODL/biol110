Slide 1
The proteins are a structurally very diverse group of macromolecules but they are all comprised of a group of 20 similar monomers.  The monomers are collectively known as amino acids.  An amino acid is a central carbon atom covalently bonded to an amino group, a carboxyl group, and a variable group indicated with the letter R.

Slide 2
The variable position is filled with one of these 20 different R groups to make 20 different amino acids.

The R-groups can be classified as being either nonpolar, polar or electrically charged.  The nonpolar groups behave hydrophobically - they are repelled by water.

The polar and electrically charged R groups behave hydrophilically - they are attracted to water.

Later we will see how these hydrophobic and hydrophilic interactions can result in either normal function of a protein and optimal health or abnormal protein function and serious health problems. 

Slide 3
Amino acids link together to produce a polypeptide - also known as a protein.  Proteins are long polymers of amino acids and just as we saw with the steriods - subtle changes in structure can result in dramatic changes in function.

Slide 4
While proteins are long chains of amino acids - they typically do not remain in a linear structure.  Instead, most protiens fold in precise 3-dimensional shapes.  It is these very precise three dimensional shapes that give a protein its precise function.

Slide 5
The nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides.  The nucleic acids include the genetic material DNA and RNA.

A nucleotide is a central sugar bonded to a nitrogenous base and a phosphate group.

The central sugar can be either deoxyribose (as it is in DNA nucleotides) or ribse (as it is in RNA nucleotides).  

The nitrogenous bases can be either Adenine, guanine, cytosine or thymine in DNA.  An RNA molecule has the same nitrogenous bases except RNA uses Uracil instead of Thymine.

Slide 6
DNA molecules are double stranded molecules that are conneceted via hydrogen bonds between complementary nitrogenous bases.  The base pairing rules are that Adenine pairs with thymine and guanine pairs with cytosine.  Understanding these base pairing rules will be important when we look at the structure and function of DNA in more detail later in the course..
