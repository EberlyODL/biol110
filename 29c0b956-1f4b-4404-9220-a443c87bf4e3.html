As we continue our survey of earth's biodiversity, our next group of organisms are the members of Kingdom Fungi.

Slide 2

There are six major groups of fungi.

The microsporidians are the newest members of Kingdom Fungi and they are all anmals parasites.  We believe this group is monophyletic but as we learn more about them - this may change.  

The Chytridiomycota (or "Chytrids") are a group of flagellated fungi - many of which are parasites.   We now think this group is paraphyletic and most likely will be reorganized.  

The Zygomycota includes common organisms such as bread mold - you know, the stuff that grows on your bread when you leave it on the counter too long.   This group is also paraphyletic and will be reorganized.

The remaining three groups are all thought to be monophyletic and include the glomeromycota (an important group of plant symbionts - we will talk more about them later).  

Phylum Ascomycota are commonly referred to as the cup or the sac fungi because they have a cup or sac shaped fruiting body.  

The Basidiomycota are known as the club fungi because they have club-shaped fruiting bodies.

Just like the plants and animals, the Fungi evolved from a protist ancestor - specifically single-celled protists with flagella.

Slide 3

Fungi live in a variety of habitats and have a variety of modes of obtaining nutrients - but they are all chemoheterotrophs - just like us.

Some fungi are even predatory like this netatode-trapping fungus.  Nematodes are smal worm-like creatures that live in the soil and other environments.

Nematode-trapping fungi develop loop traps that they use to trap nematodes as they move through the soil.  They then eat these nematodes - but instead of ingesting them the way an animal predator would - they digest the worms alive outside the fungal body and absorb the nutrients back over their cell walls.

Slide 4

Kingdom Fungi contains the largest known organism.

It is a Basidiomycete fungus that measures 3.5 miles across and extends an average of three feet into the ground.  It is found in eastern Oregon.

This fungus covers an area as big as 1,665 football fields  and is 2400 years old.

It is a parasite on trees and eventually kills the trees it is associated with.

Slide 5

Characteristics of Kingdom Fungi include that they are mainly multicellular (with the exception of the yeasts which are single celled fungi)

They are all heterotrophic (specifically chemoheterotrophs) and can be parasitic, a saprobe, or, as we have just seen, predatory. Saprobes are decomposers - they break down already dead organic material whereas parasites feed on living organic material - that is the distinction between a saprobe and a parasite.

The Fungi are what are described as absorptive heterotrophs.  They feed by secreting digestive enzymes into their food and then absorb the nutrients into the cells.  This is in contrast to the animals which are ingestive heterotrophs - we have to ingest our food and then digest it.

Fungi have cell walls made of chitin and most of them are nonmotile - meaning they do not have flagellated cells.

Many have a dikaryon state (1N + 1N) -  2 haploid nuclei in same cytoplasmic compartment - the can exist for an extended period of time during the life cycle and it is unique to the fungi.
 

Slide 6

The bodies of multicellular fungi are made up of structures known as hyphae - slender filaments that have a large surface area for secreting digestive enzymes over and absorbing nutrients.

Some species of fungi can digest cellulose and lignin so they are important recyclers in forest ecosystems.

Unfortunately this ability also makes them problematic if they start growing in your house and digesting your cellulose-based dry wall and the lignin the wood that holds your house together.

Hyphae can be either septate - when there are walls or septa that divide the hypha, or coenocytic if the hypha is not divided.

Slide 7

When we look at a mushroom, for example, we are seeing a mass of hyphae collected together into a structure known as a mycelium.

Slide 9
Fungi are involved in some important mutualistic symbiotic relationships. 

Mycorrhizae are an association between a fungus and plant roots.  This relationship is very important in helping plants to survive in the terrestrial environment.  The fungus benefits by getting sugars from the plant and the plant benefits by being better able to take up water and nutrients from the environment.

Lichen are a symbiotic relationship between a fungus and a photosynthetic partner (a green algae or cyanobacteria)

Just as with the mycorrhizae - the fungus gets fixed carbon (or sugars) and the photosynthetic partner gets minerals and water. So both of these are mutualistic relationships.  

Slide 10

Reproduction in the fungi is quite different from other organisms.

For one thing, the fungi are haploid throughout most of their life cycle.

For another, most fungi can reproduce both sexually and asexually and often only reproduce sexually when stressed.

Each of the four main groups of fungi produce distinct features during sexual reproduction and these features are used to classify the fungi.

Slide 10

To understand the fungal life cycle, there are some vocabulary words you need to know.

First, there are no sexes in the fungi because they do not produce gametes.  Instead, the fungi are or of mating types, either + or -.  The distinct strains are compatible in sexual reproduction and produce a zygote.  In other words, only a + and - can reproduce together - two positives can't reproduce and two negatives can't reproduce.

The term Plasmogamy refers to the  fusion of cytoplasm of two (or more) cells to form either a dikaryon (if two cells fuse) or a heterokaryon (if more than two cells fuse). 

The term karyogamy refers to the fusion of nuclei during sexual reproduction.

Slide 11

During sexual reproduction in the fungi, two haploid mycelia of opposite mating type grow toward each other and during the process of plasmogamy fuse together to produce the dikaryotic stage.  The dikaryon is two haploid nuclei in the same cytoplasmic compartment.

The dikaryon then goes through karyogamy - fusion of the nuclei to produce a diploid nucleus.

This starts the diploid stage.  The diploid stage is typically very brief and meiosis occurs to produce haploid spores.

These spores are then released from the parent fungus and germinate into new individuals.

As we look at the different groups of fungi - we will see that it is the dikaryon or heterokaryon stage that is unique between the different groups.
